    Name: Belaro
    Pronunciation: BELL-ah-ro
    Scientific Name: Felis crenarata
    Race: Anthro
    Height: 3-4' (0.8-1.2 m)
    Associated Elements: None

    Etymology: Unknown

    Characteristics: Belaros are mostly cat-like in appearance. They are easily separated from their other feline counterparts by their ears; belaro ears are long and ornate with a distinctive notch toward the middle. Their tails are not only generally long but also sport a notch toward the end. Their fur tends to be monochromatic. They have a mostly white coat with black stripes along their face, tail, forearms, and paws.

    Abilities: Like most other felines, they have sharp, retractable claws on all their digits. Belaros tend to be very skilled singers. In fact some of the most famous musical artists in the world and throughout history have been of the belaro species. Some belaros mix magic with their natural singing ability to cast unique spells.

    Habitat: Belaro population distribution does not tend to differ from that of other Anthros. However, they do prefer large towns and cities slightly more.

    Behavior: Belaros like beautiful things. As naturally distinguished singers, they like good music -- especially good singing voices. Even in their early history, musicians were held in high regard by belaros. They lauded anyone with a better singing voice than they had. Belaros also have an eye for shiny, glittering objects. Gold and jewelry is very popular with belaros. The object they hold most dear to them, however, are bells. They enjoy adorning themselves with cat bells and will usually hang them from the notches in their ears or tail. A belaro's golden bell is usually their most prized possession. Historically, a belaro is known to fight tooth-and-nail if their bell gets stolen.

    Diet: Belaro diets do not tend to differ from other feline Anthro species. They tend to eat more fish and poultry.

    Language: Belaros have spoken Common since its inception. Before that, they would speak whichever language was prominent in their local culture. In early history, some primarily belaro establishments spoke their own language (a modified version of the Earth Italian language.)
